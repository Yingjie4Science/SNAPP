# Copy-paste prompt for ChatGPT — societal cost per case of depression

Paste the block below directly into ChatGPT (enable web browsing for best
results). It is pre-filled for MDD / US / 2024 USD.

---

I need a defensible estimate of the **full annual societal cost per prevalent case
of major depressive disorder (MDD) in the United States, in 2024 US dollars**. I
will use it as the "societal cost per case" (health_cost_rate) in an ecosystem-
services model, so I need one recommended number plus a sensitivity range.

Please do NOT rely on a single study. Pool several cost-of-illness studies and
cross-validate. Use web browsing / literature search if available; if you can't
browse, say so and lower your confidence.

Do this, showing your work at each step:

1. Search at least two independent databases and cross-check them — PubMed/Europe
   PMC AND Google Scholar (or Semantic Scholar) — for peer-reviewed studies on the
   economic burden / cost of illness of MDD in the US, including indirect
   (workplace absenteeism + presenteeism) costs, and at least one systematic
   review or meta-analysis. Confirm the databases return the same top studies.
   Good starting points include the Greenberg et al. economic-burden series (2015
   for 2010 data; 2021 for 2018; 2023 for 2019) and the König et al. 2019
   meta-analysis "The excess costs of depression." (Note: arXiv and
   bioRxiv/medRxiv do NOT carry this literature — don't rely on them.)

2. Keep studies that report a national incremental (excess) burden AND the number
   of US adults with MDD, so a per-case figure can be derived. Prefer ≥2 different
   base years for cross-validation. Note each study's base year and the
   currency/base-year of its dollars.

3. Derive per case = total incremental burden ÷ number of US adults with MDD.
   State the denominator and its source for each study.

4. Inflation-adjust every per-case figure to 2024 USD using BLS CPI-U annual
   averages; state the index values and the factor you used.

5. Pool: take the mean of the per-case estimates from the most recent
   comparable studies, and use older vintages as a convergence check. Report the
   spread across studies and explain the main driver of any difference (e.g. the
   population denominator vs. a real change in cost).

6. Break the cost down into components (direct medical, workplace/productivity,
   suicide-related, household/caregiver, comorbidity) with each component's share.

7. Reconcile with the direct-medical-only figure from MEPS ("mean expenditure per
   person with care" for depression, ≈ $1,800/treated case): explain why the
   societal total is much larger and which component MEPS maps to.

8. State caveats: prevalence- vs incidence-based costing; per-prevalent vs
   per-treated case; comorbidity attribution and its effect on the range; whether
   one research group dominates the evidence; and geographic transferability
   (local wages shift the workplace share).

Deliverables:
- A recommended central value and an explicit low–high sensitivity range, with a
  one-line justification for each bound.
- A table of the pooled studies: citation, base year, total burden, currency-year,
  population denominator, per-case as-reported, per-case in 2024 USD, and whether
  it was pooled or convergent-only.
- A short methods paragraph (search + inclusion + inflation + pooling).
- Full citations with DOIs/links; label any number you derived yourself.
- State which databases you searched and whether their top hits agreed
  (cross-check); if you couldn't browse, say so and lower your confidence.

Present the range, not just the point estimate, as the finding.
